1. Architecture of Direct and Indirect Taxes

A Wholly Foreign-Owned Enterprise (WFOE) in China is subject to a multi-tier tax system. The key taxes are:

  • Corporate Income Tax (CIT) – Standard rate: 25%. Reduced rates apply for qualifying entities:
  • High and New Technology Enterprise (HNTE): 15% (requires certification, common in manufacturing).
  • Small Low-Profit Enterprises: progressive rates (e.g., 5% on first RMB 3 million taxable income, subject to thresholds).
  • Value-Added Tax (VAT) – Standard rate: 13% for manufacturing and goods sales. Lower rates: 9% (e.g., utilities, transport), 6% (services). Export of goods is generally zero-rated (with refund mechanism).
  • Withholding Taxes (WHT) – On outbound payments:
  • Dividends: 10% (reduced to 5% under most tax treaties, e.g., with Germany, France, UK, if beneficial owner conditions met).
  • Interest, royalties, technical service fees: 10% (treaty rates may reduce to 5–10%).
  • No WHT on dividends if the WFOE is a resident enterprise distributing to another Chinese resident enterprise.
  • Other taxes: Urban Maintenance and Construction Tax (7% of VAT paid), Education Surcharge (3% + 2% local), Stamp Duty (0.03%–0.1% on contracts), Property Tax (1.2% of original value or 12% of rental income), Land Appreciation Tax (on property transfers).

Note: Rates are statutory; actual effective rates depend on incentives, treaties, and local policies.

2. Key Legal Framework

  • Foreign Investment Law (FIL) – Effective 2020. Replaces three old FIE laws. Establishes national treatment for foreign investors, except on the Negative List.
  • Company Law – Governs corporate governance, shareholder rights, board structure. WFOEs are typically limited liability companies (LLCs). Key requirements: at least one director, one supervisor (or audit committee), annual shareholder meeting.
  • Negative List – Published by NDRC/MOFCOM. Lists sectors where foreign investment is prohibited or restricted. Manufacturing is largely open, but certain sub-sectors (e.g., rare earths, tobacco, certain chemicals) may have restrictions. Check latest list (2024 edition).
  • Annual Compliance Obligations:
  • Annual Report to SAMR (by June 30) – includes financials, shareholder info, business activities.
  • CIT Annual Filing (by May 31) – with transfer pricing documentation.
  • Foreign Investment Information Report (to MOFCOM via online system) – quarterly or annually, depending on size.
  • Labor Contract Renewal and social insurance filings (monthly).
  • Environmental Compliance (for manufacturing) – annual self-monitoring reports to local EPB.

3. Supervisory Authorities and Their Roles

  • State Administration of Taxation (SAT) – National tax policy, CIT/VAT/WHT rules. Local tax bureaus (Shanghai, Wuxi) handle collection, audits, and refunds.
  • Ministry of Finance (MOF) – Sets accounting standards (Chinese GAAP), tax treaty negotiations, fiscal policy.
  • State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) – Company registration, annual reporting, anti-monopoly review. Local AMRs handle day-to-day.
  • Ministry of Commerce (MOFCOM) – Foreign investment policy, Negative List enforcement, foreign investment information reporting.
  • National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC) – Approves foreign investments in restricted sectors, oversees industrial policies (e.g., “Made in China 2025” incentives).
  • Local Tax Bureaus – Under SAT. Conduct routine tax audits, VAT refund processing, transfer pricing reviews.
  • Other relevant bodies:
  • Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security (MOHRSS) – Labor law, social insurance.
  • Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) – Data security, PIPL enforcement.
  • Customs – Export VAT refunds, import duties, bonded zone operations.

4. Recurring Risk Areas for a Manufacturing WFOE

  • Transfer Pricing (TP) – High audit risk. Must prepare Contemporaneous TP Documentation (Master File, Local File, Country-by-Country Report if thresholds met). Key focus: related-party transactions (raw material imports, finished goods exports, management fees, royalties). Arm’s length principle strictly enforced. Benchmarking studies required.
  • Export VAT Refunds – Manufacturing WFOEs often claim VAT refunds on exported goods. Common issues: mismatched invoices, incorrect HS codes, delays in refund processing. Risk of fraud investigations if documentation incomplete.
  • Dividend Repatriation – Requires board resolution, audited financials, proof of tax payment (CIT). WHT treaty relief requires beneficial owner declaration and tax residency certificate from home country. Risk: denial of treaty benefits if substance lacking.
  • Data / PIPL (Personal Information Protection Law) – Manufacturing WFOEs collect employee data, customer data, and possibly production data. Must:
  • Obtain consent for data processing.
  • Conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments for high-risk activities.
  • Cross-border data transfer requires security assessment (if critical data) or standard contractual clauses.
  • Penalties: up to 5% of annual revenue or RMB 50 million.
  • Labor Law – Key risks:
  • Fixed-term contracts – after two renewals, employee can demand open-ended contract.
  • Social insurance – must be paid in full (pension, medical, unemployment, work injury, maternity). Underpayment triggers back-payment + fines.
  • Termination – statutory severance (1 month per year of service). Unlawful dismissal = double compensation.
  • Trade unions – WFOEs with >25 employees must have a union (or allow one to be formed). Collective bargaining rights exist.
  • Environmental Compliance – Manufacturing WFOEs must hold pollutant discharge permits, conduct regular emissions monitoring, and comply with Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) requirements. Non-compliance can lead to shutdown orders.
